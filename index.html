Scientific References

This analyzer computationally models three core real-time algorithms to evaluate vocal quality:

  • Harmonic Cleanliness: Measures variance between detected upper harmonic peaks and mathematically perfect integer multiples of the fundamental frequency (F0). A stable, purely anchored pitch hits 100%.
  • NHR Ratio: Calculates the energetic ratio of chaotic spectral noise (breath, hoarseness, hiss) against pure harmonic resonance. An NHR ratio under 0.5 indicates a pristine, non-breathy phonation.
  • Harmonic Richness: A custom consonance algorithm that rewards the amplitude of overtones matching perfect musical intervals (Octaves=1.0, Fifths=0.9, Thirds=0.95). High percentages indicate a bright, "pingy" resonance often linked to the singer's formant.

These algorithmic structures and their respective color-coded normative health thresholds correlate directly with the following clinical literature:

  • Yumoto, E., Sasaki, Y., & Okamura, H. — Harmonics-to-noise ratio and psychophysical measurement of the degree of hoarseness — Relevant for identifying pathological thresholds (HNR > 7.4 dB).
  • Boersma, P. (1993) — Accurate short-term analysis of the fundamental frequency and the harmonics-to-noise ratio of a sampled sound — Foundational method for HNR calculation in Praat.
  • Brockmann-Bauser, M. et al. — Research on jitter/shimmer/NHR algorithms and influencing factors (SPL, voice usage).
  • Marques de Medeiros, M. et al. (2006) — Standardization of acoustic measures for normal voice patterns — Direct source for normative values: jitter (0.49–0.62%) and HNR (~9.5–11 dB).
  • Sederholm, E. et al. (1999) — Acoustic analysis of the normal voice in nonsmoking adults — Source for jitter threshold < 0.5% and healthy HNR at 95th percentile ~18 dB.
  • de Araújo Pernambuco, L. et al. — Noise-to-Harmonics Ratio as an Acoustic Measure of Voice Disorders in Boys — Source for the 1.92 odds ratio for every 0.01 increase in NHR.
  • Orlikoff, R.F. (2003) — Harmonics-to-Noise Ratio: An Index of Vocal Aging — Source for normal adult HNR ranges of 11–13 dB.
